Library Website

The Gottesman Libraries at Teachers College Columbia University is constantly evolving and headed to a future of seamless experiences for patrons. The new Library App and updated Library Website are two significant steps on this journey.


New Gottesman Libraries homepage

The project:

Update the existing website to enable patrons to easily access various library services and resources. Also update the various sub-systems and integrated systems.


Old Gottesman Libraries homepage


My role:

As the lead user experience designer, I was responsible for the complete creative direction of the project, from conception and research to design and implementation.


The outcome:

The library succesfully migrated to the new systems, including NEW -

  • Library Website
  • CMS [Login Wall]
  • Room Reservation System [Login Wall]
  • Support Ticket System
  • Payments Gateway
  • Revamped WebPAC PRO (Innovative Interfaces Inc) Search System
  • Revamped Serial Solutions Search System

The Process (highlights):

    • Ideation Sessions
    • Determine Roadmap

RESEARCH:

➝ Initial Research and Interviews:

  • Met with stakeholders at the Library and University.
    • The new system should support self-directed learning.
  • Interviewed patrons to get feedback on the existing library website
    • There is too much going on throughout the site!

    Too many links


  • Did a comparative analysis of other library websites and apps.
  • Evaluated the existing website and Google Analytics data.

    • What 90% of the visitors want

➝ Contextual Inquiry with Librarians:

  • Observed and interviewed extensively (along with a UX researcher) the current 'interface' between patrons and the library - the librarians and library staff.
  • The help desk/feedback system had a lot of insightful submissions over the years.
  • The old website hasn't changed much in almost a decade so a lot of the site's flaws were well known to the library service team members.
    • Librarians would tell patrons to click the 1st column, 7th row, 2nd link, to get to the fine payment system!
  • The library staff empathizes with what patrons are looking for and gave valuable insight into how they are currently being helped.

    • A unique aspect of the library is the acquisitions are largely patron-driven. We should highlight this in the new system.

  • The Patron, who avails of various library services.
  • The Professor, who requires the library to purchase books and needs tointegrate courses with these course readings.

Jaguar Land Rover

Jaguar Land Rover's future technology research team's mission is to improve the user experience across a range of features within JLR automobiles, centered on the In-Vehicle Infotainment (IVI) system.


Testing setup at JLR's Portland research center

Image Source

The project:

Rethink JLR's In-Vehicle Infotainment systems and explore future product concepts and usages in a highly inter-connected, Internet-of-things, augmented reality world.


My Role:

As a User Experience designer, I was responsible for designing and developing IVI and surrounding experiences based on an intimate understanding of consumer electronics, apps and web concepts and the automotive context. I also developed functional HTML prototypes. These would be presented regularly to our clients and stakeholders. Finally, I would work with UX researchers to analyze feedback from user testing and focus group sessions; revisit the designs and summarize the design recommendations for each use case.


The outcome:

Our team successfully delivered the scenarios, prototypes, findings, analysis, and recommendations for multiple use cases to JLR. The details of this project are under an NDA, so if you’d like to learn more, please ask offline.

The Process (Highlights):

  • STRATEGY
    • Brainstorm IVI use cases
  • RESEARCH
    • Explore and develop usage models and scenarios
  • DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE
    • Design and develop high-fidelity, functional prototypes for testing in cars
  • DELIVERABLES
    • Create detailed interaction design specs
    • Provide the team with a feasibility analysis and design recommendations.

STRATEGY:

➝ Ideation Sessions / Stakeholder Meetings:

  • Brainstormed various ideas that could be explored with current and potential JLR clients.
  • Met with JLR employees to learn about their vision and expectations.

➝ Project Requirements:

  • Each use case concept needed to be completely detailed out, with user models, scenarios, storyboards, design intent, and recommendations, functioning prototypes, and high fidelity visual designs.

Design Thinking Sessions

I was personally involved in the following use case concepts over the course of two years-

    • Updated media experiences (front and back seat)

Rhizr

Rhizr is a collection of learning opportunities linked together to support serendipitous discovery. Use it to create, organize, reuse, and remix content.


Responsive Rhizr Designs

The project:

Design a new learning and organization interface.


Rhizomatic learning is a way of thinking about learning based on ideas described by Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari. A rhizome is a stem of a plant that sends out roots and shoots as it spreads. Similarly, in Rhizomatic learning, ideas are multiple, interconnected and self-replicating.

My role:

This was one of my first projects at EdLab. As a user experience designer, I used this opportunity to introduce various UX methods to the team. Then as a user interface developer, I also helped develop this project. I worked with a project manager and two developers on this project.


The Outcome:

  • Rhizr was successfully released to a limited audience for analysis, testing, tracking usage patterns and to populate with content.

The Process (Highlights):

  • STRATEGY
    • Analysis of initial direction
  • RESEARCH
    • Design thinking sessions
    • Brainstorming and ideation sessions
    • Interviewing potential users and creating user models
  • DESIGN AND IMPLEMENT
    • Design wireframes
    • UI development
    • Usability testing
  • NEXT STEPS
    • Iterate

STRATEGY:

➝ Dissect 'mSchool':

  • Rhizr had started off as 'mSchool', a project about learning through metaphors. It had hit a roadblock as the initial ideas around it were found to be insufficient and it needed a new creative direction.

➝ Brainstorming / Design thinking:

  • With the help of the product manager, the dev team and some design thinking sessions we came up with a revised idea for the project and a new name, 'Rhizr.'
Rhizr strives to create "a learning environment in which the student can learn to restructure the new information and their prior knowledge into new knowledge about the content and to practice using it" - Dean A. McManus

Concept of Rhizr

RESEARCH:

➝ Determining User Needs / Task Flows:

  • Spoke to educators, students, and researchers about e-learning platforms, online learning, creating and sharing learning content online.
  • We determined there are potentially three primary types of users for Rhizr - the creator, the passive learner, and the active learner.

New user models

➝ Determining User Needs / Task Flows:

  • I mapped out user needs and goals and created task flows.

A Task flow - Creating a new Rhizr

DESIGN AND IMPLEMENT:

➝ Information Architecture / Interaction Design:

  • We analyzed different layouts, including hierarchical layout, topological layouts, various grid and canvas layouts, before we settled on the simple column layout.

Wireframe of a Rhizr with Nodes and Roots

➝ Bootstrap Framework + Meteor JavaScript:

  • As we were a small team and since I have UI development experience, I helped in developing the beta version using the Bootstrap framework and Meteor JavaScript platform.

➝ Testing:

  • We have been collecting a lot of feedback and data from the beta version.
    • The metaphorical structure of the website i.e the concept of Rhizrs, Roots, and Nodes is not very understandable to users.
  • Next step is to analyze all the feedback we have got and revisit the designs and the core idea of Rhizr.
    • The ideas around 'curating' and 'remixing' are not very clear to users and the functionality is also not very intuitive.

Viewing a 'Rhizr'

  • Project: Rhizr

Vialogues

"Vialogues", deriving from video + dialogues, is a community of learners using video to ignite discussion.

NEW RESPONSIVE SITE

The project:

Redesign and enhance Vialogues with a focus on self-directed learning. With thousands of users around the world still using its first incarnation (launched in 2010), there has been a desperate need for an upgrade and constant demand for additional features and an easier to use interface.


Old Vialogues Homepage


My role:

I was the lead designer on this project and was responsible for its complete creative execution. I worked closely with a UX researcher for the initial analysis and later testing parts, and with a visual designer to create the high-fidelity designs.


The outcome:

  • Vialogues was launched on 10th August 2017 to rave reviews and feedback!
  • There is now better discoverability of people and content.
  • There is an increased emphasis on groups of related content.
  • The new design fixed a lot of the usability and findability issues of the previous version.
  • The site is now less "busy" and the design allows the content to have breathing room.

The Process (Highlights):

  • STRATEGY
    • Determining areas of improvement
    • Determine project scope
  • RESEARCH
    • Evaluation of existing platform
    • Interview and observe current users
  • DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE
    • Wireframe new designs
    • Collect feedback on high-fidelity mockups
    • Creative direction of visual design
  • IMPLEMENT
    • CSS implementation

STRATEGY:

  • Create a more robust discussion environment
    • How do we help create the rich, active discussions we set out to support and provide users the sense of community they feel across similar discussion sites?
  • Provide moderators with data and analytics about their vialogues
  • Improve assessment methods
    • How can we improve our polling and question feature to better serve learners?
  • Improve the search and recommendation
    • How can we improve discoverability of people and content?
  • General update of the UI
    • Update overall style / icons / fonts / visual elements
    • Reduce clutter / Update page layouts / Give breathing room
    • Expose hard to find features (Ex: video settings)
    • Simplify complicated functionality (Ex: create process)

➝ Determining Project Scope:

For our first phase, we will be focusing on general UI updates and improve the search & recommendations system.

      • How do I change the closed-captions?

    • We interviewed several existing users of Vialogues, including -
      • EdLab staff, who use it for video hosting/streaming

EdLab Website

EdLab is a research, design, and development unit at Teachers College, Columbia University working on the future of education technology, but they had a website that was stuck in the early 2000's.


New Responsive Site

The project:

Design a new website for EdLab highlighting EdLab's network of apps by enabling users to easily interact with the enormous amount of content that users create using these apps.


My role:

I was the lead designer on this project and was responsible for its complete creative execution. I worked closely with a UX researcher for the initial analysis and later testing parts, and supervised a visual designer to create the high-fidelity designs. I then worked closely with the developers during the implementation stage, including help polish some functionality and styles by diving into the code.


The Outcome:

  • The new site was launched in March 2017.
  • We revised the IA, simplified the navigation, came up with a simple and clean design aesthetic.
  • The eventual final design and layout was liked enough by our stakeholders to make it "our look" and became the start of unifying the design across our different websites and apps.
  • This project also led to the creation of other related projects, like EdLab Global Search, the EdLab CMS project, integrated EdLab authentication system.

The Process (Highlights):

  • STRATEGY
    • Original scope
  • RESEARCH
    • Evaluation of existing website
    • New scope
    • Creating a sitemap of the existing website
    • Determine user needs
  • DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE
    • Develop new information architecture
    • Wireframe new designs and create high-fidelity mockups
  • IMPLEMENT
    • Create transition plan for content
    • Create design specs for developers
    • CSS implementation
    • Usability testing

RESEARCH:

➝ Project scope and requirements:

  • Our initial focus and assignment was to simply 're-skin' the old website to make it look nicer and cleaner.
  • As we went down the path of a 're-skin', we realized there were several things about the site that didn't work.
  • Eventually, the scope expanded to build a completely new website with new requirements and features.

The Old Homepage


➝ Evaluation of old website:

  • The main user base for the website is the EdLab staff, who use it to write blog posts for internal/external audiences, create events, advertise new job positions, update people information, and highlight projects and work.
  • Several visitors to the EdLab website originate from one of our different apps, trying to create new EdLab app accounts.
    • Since the old EdLab website had a different account system compared to other EdLab apps, confused users created EdLab 'website accounts' instead of an EdLab 'apps account'.
  • Analyzed traffic data to determine popular pages and paths.
  • Created a sitemap and explored the source repository to map the hundreds of pages and sub-pages within the old website.

DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE:

➝ Interviews / Design events:

  • Spoke to several EdLab staff members to get their feedback on the current website.
  • Organized design events to get ideas from the Teachers College community.
    • The larger Teachers College community members didn't know much about EdLab.

➝ Information Architecture / Interaction Design:

  • We felt the site needs to be a central point for "everything EdLab", and not just be an 'About Us' brochure site.
  • Landing page structure: We settled on a structure for the new website that had three distinct sections -
    • DO: This section would be an entry point to our network of apps that users can experience through a super search, links to apps, or a single EdLab account creation process.
    • SHOW: To give users a taste of EdLab, this would be a section to highlight our recent and upcoming activities and featured content (from different apps).
    • TELL: Now that users have tried out what we have to offer and have seen what we can do, we wanted to tell them a little more about us in this section.

New site structure

  • When we initially worked on re-skinning the site, it was a period of management and the design team understanding each other's processes and design sensibilities and aesthetic sense.
  • Some of our initial designs were very visceral and visually bright and attractive -- while our stakeholders liked them, they felt it did not convey their feelings towards EdLab.
  • Our later design iterations were more simple and clean.

Early visual design option


➝ Prototyping:

  • We used InVision app to create, share, and test our prototypes.
  • Constantly tested our designs with members of the Teachers College community and iterated based on their feedback.

    • The landing page reflected the internal organization structure as each team wanted a piece of the pie, leading to an information architecture that did not make any sense to customers.

DESIGN + PROTOTYPE:

➝ Information Architecture:

We brainstormed multiple approaches to improve the IA, including -

  • Product Consolidation - bring all products under one section and help users find what they are looking for by 'Product Type' (ex: Laptop).
  • Franchise Branding and Activities - allow users to shop by 'Brands' (ex: Latitude or Alienware) or 'Activity' (ex: Gaming).

    • Initial design


  • Desegmentation - We decided to proceed with desegmentation and test which of the following reduced segments will work best:

    OPTION 1: Single segment


    OPTION 2: Two main business segments


    OPTION 3: Partial Desgmentation

➝ Testing:

  • I created hi-fi interactive mockups of the desegmentation options in Axure.
  • The three options were tested against each other and existing Dell.com (control).
  • The users we recruited belonged to three categories - consumer, business, and crossover.
  • We had 6 participants per option per category for a total of 72 participants.
  • Each participant had 7 browsing tasks and 4 follow-up survey questions.

➝ Results and Learnings:

  • Consumer users favored Option 2 (For Home, For Work) the most.
  • Crossover users also favored Option 2 (For Home, For Work) compared to other options.
  • Consumer and Crossovers disliked the existing dell.com.
    • Users felt the home page was cluttered and struggled to find the correct place to start engaging with the navigation.
  • Unlike others, Business users had the most success on the control (dell.com), followed by Option 1 (Products).
  • The study results support the hypothesis that desegmentation is the right direction.
  • Overall, the most favored option was Option 2, or reducing to two segments - 'For Home' and 'For Work'.

LAUNCH:

This was implemented in March 2013. Read Dell's blog post for more details.

Note: The Dell homepage has since been updated to Option 1 (only 'Products') for USA and Europe visitors. It remains in Option 2 ('For Home', 'For Work') for some International markets.

Evolve Outlet

The Analytical Product Design course at The University of Michigan requires students to work in teams to apply multidisciplinary (engineering design, product design, human-centered design, and product-directed entrepreneurship) methods on a project. We redesigned the standard power outlet.


The Evolve Outlet

The Project:

Redesign the standard power outlet considering advancements in technology, increased user demand, and new user requirements.


My Role:

Our team was a multi-disciplinary team, consisting of a mechanical engineer (Cole), an electrical engineer (Karthik), a business major (Dan) and a user experience designer (me). As the user experience lead, I was actively involved in the research and design phases.


The Outcome:

We designed an award-winning outlet, with multiple sockets, USB charging ports, and an extension cord -- all built into a standard size gang-box with an unobtrusive design.

The Process (Highlights):

  • STRATEGY
    • Ideation & brainstorming
  • RESEARCH
    • User interviews
    • Conducting surveys
    • Persona generation
    • Comparative analysis
  • DESIGN AND PROTOTYPE
    • Sketching and prototyping
    • User testing

DESIGN EVOLUTION:

➝ Research:

  • We interviewed and observed our friends and classmates about their outlet usage in their homes, dorms, and apartments.
  • We also observed general usage at more public spaces, like coffee shops and classrooms.
  • The most common issues we identified were:
    • Inaccessible outlets - either located far away or behind some obstruction
    • Not enough sockets - already occupied by other chargers
    • Lack of USB ports for newer devices - need to use an extra adapter

➝ Design:

  • We came up with several design ideas and sketches that contained various combinations of multiple outlets and USB outlets.
  • Some of these ideas were the replacement of the whole outlet gang-box, while others were plug-and-use models.
  • We also tried to create a 'light-enabled' adapter that goes on to the end of a user's charging cable to help when visibility is poor around the wall outlet.

Evolution of our design

➝ Prototypes and Testing:

  • We initially created cardboard and paper prototypes of some of these designs and tested them out.
  • We got the idea to enhance the design to include an extension cord inside during this testing phase.

➝ Final Design:

The 'Evolve Outlet' had the following features-

  • 4 Standard Power Outlets
  • 2 USB Charging Ports
  • Self-contained, auto-retracting 48" extension cord.
  • Fits in the standard 2-Panel wall box
A 3D print version of the evolve outlet was displayed at the Design Expo at The University of Michigan and won the Best Project Award! The project was also displayed at UMSI's "expoSItion '13" and got an honorable mention under the overall best project category.
